What Is a QR Code?

A QR code (Quick Response code) is a two-dimensional barcode that stores information in a square grid of black-and-white pixels. Unlike a traditional barcode, which only holds data in a single line, a QR code stores data both horizontally and vertically, allowing it to hold far more information in a much smaller space.

QR codes are used in:

  • Marketing and advertising
  • Restaurant menus
  • Payment systems
  • Product packaging
  • Event ticketing
  • Business cards
  • WiFi sharing
  • Educational materials
  • App downloads

Every day, millions of smartphones scan QR codes to instantly open websites, view menus, connect to WiFi, or save contact details. Modern QR code generators support multiple data types for marketing, business, and personal use.

Common QR Code Types

Website / URL QR Code

One of the most popular QR code types.

Best for:

  • Redirecting users to a website
  • Linking to landing pages
  • Sharing online menus
  • Marketing campaigns

Scans open the link directly in the user's browser.

vCard / Contact QR Code

Stores contact information for quick saving to a phone.

Common uses include:

  • Business cards
  • Networking events
  • Email signatures
  • Trade shows

Plain Text QR Code

Displays a simple text message when scanned.

Used for:

Instructions

Short messages

Product information

No app or internet connection is required to read the text.

Email QR Code

Opens a pre-filled email draft when scanned.

Typically found on:

  • Customer support pages
  • Feedback requests
  • Newsletter signups

Saves users from typing out an address by hand.

WiFi QR Code

Connects a device to a WiFi network automatically when scanned, without typing a password.

Tips for Better QR Code Scanning

For reliable scanning:

  • Print at high resolution.
  • Maintain good contrast (dark code on a light background).
  • Avoid stretching or distorting the QR code.
  • Leave enough quiet space (margin) around the edges.
  • Avoid covering the corner squares with logos or text.
  • Test with a phone camera before large-scale printing.
