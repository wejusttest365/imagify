What Is a Barcode?

A barcode is a visual representation of data that can be read by a scanner or camera. It consists of parallel lines and spaces of varying widths that encode information such as product codes, prices, or inventory details.

Barcodes are used in:

  • Retail stores
  • Supermarkets
  • Warehouses
  • Logistics companies
  • Healthcare
  • Manufacturing
  • Libraries
  • Event ticketing
  • Asset management

Every day, billions of barcode scans help businesses identify products, manage inventory, and speed up checkout processes. Modern barcode systems support multiple formats for retail, logistics, and industrial applications.

Common Barcode Types

Code 128

One of the most versatile barcode formats.

Best for:

  • Inventory management
  • Shipping labels
  • Warehouse systems
  • Internal tracking

Supports letters, numbers, and symbols.

Code 39

A popular barcode for industrial applications.

Common uses include:

  • Manufacturing
  • Asset tracking
  • Government agencies
  • Automotive industry

EAN-13

The international retail barcode standard.

Used on:

Grocery products

Consumer goods

Retail packaging

Contains exactly 13 digits.

UPC-A

The standard barcode used in North American retail.

Typically found on:

  • Food products
  • Consumer electronics
  • Household goods

Contains 12 numeric digits.

EAN-8

A compact version of EAN-13 designed for small packages where label space is limited.

Tips for Better Barcode Printing

For reliable scanning:

  • Print at high resolution.
  • Maintain good contrast (black on white).
  • Avoid stretching the barcode.
  • Leave enough quiet space around the edges.
  • Test with a barcode scanner before large-scale printing.
